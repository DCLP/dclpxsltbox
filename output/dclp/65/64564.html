Introduction

Medical recipes (SB XXVI 16458 = PSI VI 718). Parchment sheet (5.6 x 7.7 cm) likely from either a short roll or a codex containing three medical prescriptions (the last line of the first one and two complete ones) and the beginning of a "geoponic" recipe to make old wine good. Marganne 2014, pp. 218-9, discusses the possibility that it could have come from a parchment notebook. The reverse side is blank, with ink traces coming from the other face. The sheet was folded three times as it appears from two extant vertical folds. Palaeography suggests a datation to the first half of the 4th cent. AD (cf. P.Vind.Sijp. 1, 338 AD). Particularly noteworthy are the paratextual devices employed: a number centered in the top margin, likely indicating a column or page count; paragraphoi separating the recipes from each other (ll. 1-2; 4-5; 12-13; the first one is not transcribed in the ed.alt. but appears correctly in the ed.pr. and in the Sammelbuch); filling marks at the end of each text block (ll. 2, 5, 12). The filling marks deserve a closer attention. The first editor (A. Olivieri, PSI VI 718) considered them as indications of weight measures (abbreviation for drachmas + number, overlined in one case: ll. 2 and 12) and tachygraphic marks (l. 5; cf. also McNamee, Abbreviations82 where it is transcribed as part of the preceding abbreviation). The second editor (F. Ronconi, SB XXVI 16458) correctly reads them as graphical devices aimed at separating different recipes and describes them as shaped in the same way: an S-shaped sign followed by a horizontal line, ending in a curl, the length of which depends on the blank space to be filled. However, from the photo of the sheet it appears clear that at the end of l. 5 we have three signs: the S-shaped one; a curled one, which resembles the preceding one pretty much; and the horizontal line. The combination of the first two is the same as ll. 2 and 12. We may infer that we are dealing with two different sets of signs: (1) a double "curl", for which the old definition of "Koronis", advanced by U. Wilcken, APF 7, 1924, 86, is quite appropriate; (2) a horizontal line acting as a filler mark. At any rate, it is surprising that the scribe filled the blank at the end of the line with this sign in one case, and simply started the next recipe at the end of l. 12.

Notes

  • 1.

    It is not clear how this figure might be the indication of the number of the recipe (ed.alt. ad loc.: "indicazione del numero della ricetta, non direi della pagina") since two complete recipes and two incomplete ones are hosted on the same sheet. It is more likely the number of column (of a roll) or page (of a codex), as Ronconi correctly states in his introduction.

  • 2.

    End of a recipe started in the preceding sheet (or column). A lost quantity of seed of something must have been taken.

  • 3-5.

    Complete recipe against enuresis: wine and grinded cyperus to be given to drink. On the astringent power of cyperus see Gal. XIII 664,3 and 881,6 K.

  • 6-12.

    Complete recipe against hoarseness: grinded bitter almonds to be applied along with oil. If the patient is aphonic and can't speak, pepper is to be added. On the use of bitter almonds to cure pneumological diseases see Alex.Trall. II 171,22-23 Puschm.

  • 7.

    κυλα κλεψε: if Ronconi's interpretation is correct, then we have to read κοῖλα ἐκλέψαι in the sense of "remove the plaques from the oro-pharyngeal cavity". According to Marganne, instead, we ought to read χυλ(ὸν) ἔκλειχε meaning "suck the juice", i.e. the eclegme (syrupy medicine) prepared according to the following recipe (cf. Antonius Musa ap. Asklepiades ap. Gal. Comp.med.sec.loc. 7,2 = XIII 47,17-48,4 K.; Ps.Gal. Remed.parab. 9 = XIV 364,11 K.; Dsc. Rem.fac. II 36,2,5; 40,2,7; 41,5,7; 41,5,5; Dsc. Mat.med. I 123,1; Orib. Eun. IV 76,1 = 466,20 Raed.).

  • 10.

    ἐκλείχειν: if Marganne's readings are correct, then it is another reference to the eclegme (see above, note to l. 7): the grinded almonds had to be mixed with oil to produce a syrup to be sucked by the patient.

  • 11.

    πέπερι: for the use of pepper in the treatment of respiratory diseases see Dsc. Mat.med. II 159.

  • 12-13.

    Beginning of the "geoponic" recipe on how to make the old wine good. No attention is paid to this last prescription by the editors, but note that aromatic wine could have had also a medical use (see e.g. J.-L. Fournet, C. Magdelaine, Liste de vins aromatisés à usage médical, in GMP I, pp. 164-70, and A. Maravela, Vina fictitia) and it seems likely that this recipe advised to spice the wine in some unknown way. This may account for its inclusion in a collection of medical prescriptions.

  • 12-13.

    Beginning of a geoponic recipe, continued on the following page (or column): "to make old wine good...".
