Introduction

The papyrus preserves parts of a single column containing 43 lines written in a small-sized informal hand, on the back of a letter. Only the right margin is partially preserved. The handwriting suggests a dating to the 2nd cent. AD, but the apostrophe used to separate double consonants may well date the text to the early 3rd cent. The text is organized into sections by headings in the form of a question: on such topic see I. Andorlini, ‘Testi medici per la scuola: raccolte di definizioni e questionari nei papiri’, in A. Garzya, J. Jouanna (eds.), Les Textes médicaux grecs: Tradition et ecdotique (Naples 1999) 7-15. Recently published parallels are GMP 1.6 (see also Hanson 2003), P.Strasb. gr. inv. 849 (ed. by Magdelaine 2004), and P.Oslo inv. 1576 (ed. by Maravela-Solbakk and Leith 2007). The question-headings, which are in eisthesis, might have been accompanied by paragraphus or diple. Horizontal strokes mark the end of answers at ll. 20 and 32. The text is a sistematic exposition of surgery consisting in divisions into its ‘forms’ and also into different classes of surgical operations. Two sections can be distinguished ll. 1-20 and 21-42. Some parallels of concious systematizations of the branch of medecine can be found e.g. in Mnesith.Ath. frr. 10-11 Bertier, Herophil. T42-49 von Staden, and Erasistr. fr. 32 Garofalo. The present text may derive from a form of introductory handbook on medicine, but the complexity of the divisions suggests a derivation from the introductory section of a book entirely devoted to surgery (see BKT III 22-26 and P.Oxy. III 437).

Notes

  • 1-20.

    The first (lost) form was presumably introduced in an initial μέν clause. There may be an indirect reference to the divisions of surgery at Ps.-Gal. Def.med. 469 [19.460 K.]. A possible introductory question is something like πόσα τὰ σχήματα τῆς χειρουργίας; The first four forms preserved by the papyrus have various parallel, see e.g. Heliod. / Antyll. ap. Orib. Coll.med. 50.3.5-9 (CMG 6.2.2.57 Raed.).

  • 2.

    μεθοδικόν probably refers to the selection of a suitable surgical procedure upon examination of the patient. A similar meaning of μέθοδος occurs in Paul.Aeg. 6.77.4 (CMG 9.2, 120 Heib.).

  • 3-7.

    Various positions for operations can be found in the literature, such as the 'seated' (Aet. Iatr. 15.5) or the 'Alexandrian' (P.Ryl. 3.529r col. 2.66-76). For the terms used here, see Gal. MM 6.4 (10.415 K.). Above the delta of [ἐπιτη]δίων there is a trace of an ascending diagonal, but it is likely not to be part of a letter.

  • 7-11.

    Different types of incisions were used depending on the parts of the body, see e.g. Antillus ap. Aet. 15.5 and Aet. 3.15 (CMG 8.1, 274 Oliv.). τομικός is a hapax legomenon. The difference between πλαγιοτομεῖν and λοξοτομεῖν is difficult to determine, unless they are used as synonyms.

  • 11-16.

    The καιρικόν form (καιρός refers to the stage reached during an affection or to opportunity or correct time for the appropriate treatment) indicates the post-operative treatements, but can refer just to the surgeon's awareness of the stages of a condition and their importance in dtermining the forms of treatment. Semiotics, i.e. the art of predicting past, present and future developement of a condition, was an essential aspect of ancient medicine, and also of surgey, see e.g. Ps.-Gal. Intro. 8 (14.693-4 K.).

  • 11-16.

    These lines concern more abstract aspects of surgery. καιρικόν indicates post-operative treatments

  • 14.

    σημιωτικόν was considered as a fundamental aspect of ancient therapeutics and surgery.

  • 16-18.

    The allusion to obstetric surgery may reflect a tendency to isolate gynaecology as a particular field of medicine (see e.g. Sor. Gyn. 3.1-5). For ἐγκατατέμνω see Hp. Foet. exsect. 1.1. About the operations to remove the foetus see Cels. De med. 7.29 and Sor. Gyn. 4.9-12. ἐγκατατομικός is a hapax legomenon.

  • 21-42.

    The section contains a separate division of surgery based on distinctions between the aims of different categories of surgical operations. The primary division is into simple and compound ways of treating, further divided into two kinds.

  • 23.

    μεθοδεύεται is palaeographically more difficult than μεθοδεύει, but gives a better meaning.

  • 26-32.

    The two simple ways are described as having a single aim, and as encompassing operations principally concerned with a patient’s appearance or with the resulting quality of life (presumably referring to physical ability). A division into simple and compound ways of curing occurs in Gal. MM 10.103-4 K. On the distinction of surgical procedures according to primarily cosmetic or corrective outcomes see P.Fuad Univ. App. I 1 i 9-13 (ed. by Marganne 1994, 147-172); for further similar distinctions see Aet. Iatr. 7.94 and Paul. Aeg. 6.19, 53, 54.

  • 33-42.

    The interpretation of the compound ways is not completely clear, but the text seems to assert that the two compound ways aim at saving the patient's life and leaving a pleasing appearance.

  • 40-42.

    Translation according to the restorations proposed in the apparatus: "[For this?] is added to a [good quality] of life or [even] to attractiveness".
