Introduction

Surgical treatise on dislocations (P.Lond.Lit. 166 + P.Cair.Mich. 2.10). The London papyrus (28,5 x 36 cm) preserves five columns of text, the first and the last in a bad state of preservation, written on the recto along the fibres from a volumen. Each column is 23,4 x 7 cm wide and contains 39 lines. The upper margin is 1,5 cm wide and the lower one about 2 cm; the intercolumnium measures 1,5 cm. Diplai for filling up the line occur five times (II 3; II 7 (preceded by a line-filler dot); III 31; IV 31), paragraphoi twice (after lines III 8; IV 6), and a diple obelismene followed by a blank space, after line V 37, probably marking the begin of a new chapter. The Michigan piece, preserving one column and traces of a second one, exhibits similar bibliological measures and features (see the diple at l. I 4 and the paragraphos at ll. II 9-10). The content of the London piece deals with four different methods to reduce the dislocation of the jaw; the first three mechanical treatments are rejected by the author due to their inefficacy. The first one (I-II 12) uses the “small astragalos” and wax; the second (II 13 - III 8) is the Alexandrine method (see also P.Ryl. II 529); the third one (III 9 - IV 6) is suitable in case of unilateral dislocation or a bilateral disarticulation of the jaw and uses two bands crossed around the neck. In the last section (IV 7 - V 37) the author displays his own method, based on the utilization of two bands crossed next to the ears and fixed one above the head and another on the ground. The Michgan fragment describes procedures for reducing both anterior and posterior shoulder dislocations. The treatise is attributed to Heliodorus with uncertainties; according to the recent hypothesis by D. Leith, the treatise on the papyrus might have been a source for Heliodorus. The hand, small informal round with cursive tendencies, could be assigned to the early 2nd century AD.
